What?

The data for compiling the database was derived from the Penn-Helsinki Parsed Corpus of Middle English (Kroch & Taylor 2000) and the Penn-Helsinki Parsed Corpus of Early Modern English (Kroch et al. 2004). These diachronic corpora cover the period from 1150-1720 and include both preand post-schwa-loss data.

How?

DECCE includes:
(a) Word forms that ended in consonant clusters at the time of their attestation,
(b) Word forms that had the potential of ending in clusters after schwa loss,
(c) Word forms that had lost a cluster which had been present in their predecessors (e.g. /klaɪm/ from /klɪ:mb/ after final consonant deletion).

Why?

Consonant clusters are phonologically speaking highly marked structures which are nevertheless abundant in English. Our aim is to enrich the scientific landscape of phonology with up to now unexplored diachronic aspects of cluster development, showing how coda clusters emerged and why they are historically speaking stable despite their marked status.
